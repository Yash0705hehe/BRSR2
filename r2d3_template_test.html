The Filing Explosion

In 2022, when SEBI first required the top 1,000 listed companies to file Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reports, only 173 companies managed to comply. The mandate was ambitious. The infrastructure wasn't ready.

Companies scrambled. Sustainability teams were assembled overnight. Consultants were hired. Data that had never been systematically collected — energy consumption, water withdrawal, waste generation, workforce demographics — suddenly needed to be quantified, verified, and published.

1,214 companies

By 2025, the picture had transformed. 1,214 companies were actively filing BRSR reports — a seven-fold increase in just three years. This isn't gradual adoption. This is a compliance avalanche.

The growth curve tells a specific story: a tentative start in 2022, an explosive catch-up in 2023 when filings jumped to 1,074, then a steady climb as the remaining companies fell into line. India now has one of the world's largest corporate sustainability disclosure ecosystems.

But filing isn't the same as substance

The EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive covers 50,000 companies. Japan's TCFD mandate reaches 4,000. China requires 1,400. India's 1,214 reporters account for just 2% of the ~50,000 listed companies — but they represent over 65% of total market capitalization.

The question that haunts this data is whether India's BRSR ecosystem is producing genuine transparency or sophisticated compliance theater. The answer, as we'll see, depends entirely on which metric you examine.

The BRSR framework encompasses nine principles derived from the National Guidelines on Responsible Business Conduct (NGRBC), aligned with the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights. It is arguably more comprehensive than most international ESG frameworks.

What makes India's trajectory remarkable is the speed. The EU took nearly a decade from the Non-Financial Reporting Directive (2014) to CSRD implementation (2024). India compressed a comparable journey into three years. The cost of that compression shows up in data quality — approximately 30% of early filings contain significant gaps in environmental metrics — but the directional momentum is unmistakable.

Nine Principles, Uneven Mastery

The BRSR framework is built on nine NGRBC principles — from ethical governance (P1) to consumer responsibility (P9). In theory, each principle receives equal weight. In practice, corporate India has created a stark hierarchy of attention.

Principles 1 through 5 — covering ethics, product safety, employee wellbeing, stakeholder engagement, and human rights — see near-universal policy adoption. These are the "comfortable" principles: they align with existing corporate governance codes, HR policies, and customer-facing commitments.

The laggard principles

Then there are the principles that expose India Inc.'s ESG blind spots. Principle 7 — public policy advocacy — remains the most neglected, with fewer than 15% of companies disclosing meaningful engagement. Principle 8 (inclusive growth) and Principle 9 (consumer value) follow close behind.

This isn't random. The avoided principles require companies to look beyond their factory walls — to engage with policy, communities, and systemic issues. They demand a shift from ESG-as-compliance to ESG-as-strategy, and most companies aren't there yet.

The manufacturing sector leads on environmental principles but lags on social ones. Financial services excels at governance but barely registers on environmental metrics. IT companies report strong workforce practices but struggle with supply chain transparency.

This sectoral divergence creates an interesting pattern: no sector has achieved balanced ESG maturity. Every industry has blind spots, and the blind spots are predictable — they correspond to the issues each sector finds most uncomfortable or operationally challenging to address.

The Sector Map

India's 1,605 unique BRSR-reporting companies span ten distinct sectors. Manufacturing dominates with 570 active filers — nearly half the total. Financial services follows with 139, then IT & Technology at 76.

The smaller sectors — real estate (29), agriculture (27), transport (25) — are where the most interesting stories hide. Small sample sizes amplify anomalies, but they also reveal patterns that get lost in the manufacturing juggernaut.

Size doesn't predict sophistication

Financial services companies, despite reporting the highest median turnover (₹37,649 Cr in 2025), have some of the weakest environmental disclosures. Their energy footprint is genuinely small — these are office-based operations — but that doesn't excuse the near-zero reporting on financed emissions, which is where their real environmental impact lies.

Conversely, the agriculture & food sector, with a modest 27 filers, reports the highest renewable energy share at 82%. Biomass and agricultural waste-to-energy pathways give this sector a structural advantage that manufacturing and energy companies spend billions trying to replicate.

Construction & infrastructure stands out for the wrong reasons: 73 worker fatalities in 2025 alone — more than any other sector. The industry's reliance on contract labor, hazardous working conditions, and historically weak safety cultures creates a persistent human cost that BRSR data is now making visible.

IT & Technology, meanwhile, presents a paradox: employee fatalities have risen from 0 to 7 over three years in what should be the safest sector. The numbers are small, but the trajectory warrants investigation — are these commuting accidents, mental health crises, or reporting artifacts?

The Human Cost

Behind every data point in this report is a human reality. Nowhere is this more apparent than in workplace safety. In 2025, BRSR-reporting companies collectively disclosed 96 employee fatalities and 263 worker fatalities.

The distinction between "employee" and "worker" is crucial. Employees are permanent staff; workers include contract labor, temporary hires, and gig workers. The fact that worker fatalities outnumber employee fatalities by nearly 3:1 reveals the two-tier safety reality of Indian industry.

263 worker deaths

Contract workers operate in the same facilities, often doing the same tasks, but with weaker safety training, less protective equipment, and fewer avenues for reporting hazards. The BRSR data doesn't just document fatalities — it documents a structural inequality in who bears physical risk in Indian industry.

The good news: total fatalities have declined from 395 in 2023 to 359 in 2025. The bad news: worker fatalities in construction haven't budged — 72 in 2023, 70 in 2024, 73 in 2025. The sector is growing, and its safety record isn't keeping pace.

The LTIFR puzzle

Lost Time Injury Frequency Rate (LTIFR) — the standard global metric for workplace safety — shows a median of 0.0 across nearly every sector. This doesn't mean Indian workplaces are miraculously safe. It means LTIFR reporting is either not being done systematically, or companies are reporting only severe incidents.

International benchmarks provide context: global manufacturing LTIFR averages 3.0-5.0 per million hours worked. India's reported median of 0.0 is statistically implausible for a universe of 1,200+ companies and suggests a fundamental measurement gap that SEBI will need to address in future BRSR revisions.

The Gender Lens

In 2025, BRSR-reporting companies employed a total of 1.81 million women across 1,156 reporting entities. This is the first time India has had a comprehensive, standardized dataset on female workforce participation across listed companies.

The distribution is starkly uneven. IT & Technology accounts for 557,765 women — nearly a third of the total — despite representing just 6% of filers. Financial services adds another 499,056. Together, these two service sectors employ 58% of all women reported in the BRSR universe.

The factory floor gap

Manufacturing, with 570 filers, reports only 231,588 women. Construction & infrastructure — 44 companies — reports just 6,200 women total. That's roughly 141 women per company, in an industry that employs millions.

The POSH (Prevention of Sexual Harassment) data tells a parallel story. IT companies reported 413 complaints across 74 companies. Financial services reported 589 across 137 companies. Construction? Just 17 complaints across 44 companies. The question isn't whether construction has fewer incidents — it's whether the mechanisms for reporting exist at all.

The aggregate POSH trajectory is actually encouraging: from 2 complaints in 2023 to 2,009 in 2025. This explosion doesn't signal a worsening workplace — it signals that grievance mechanisms are being built, awareness is being raised, and women are becoming more willing to report. Every complaint filed is a system working as intended.

Global benchmark: the UK reported over 40,000 workplace harassment complaints in 2023 across a workforce of 33 million. India's 2,009 complaints across a BRSR workforce that likely exceeds 15 million suggests significant under-reporting persists.

0%

That is the rate of independent external assurance across the entire BRSR reporting universe. Not a single sector has achieved meaningful third-party verification of its sustainability data. India's ESG disclosure ecosystem is, for now, entirely self-certified. This is the single largest credibility gap in the framework — and the one SEBI will most urgently need to close.
